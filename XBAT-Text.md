Every design delta from the reference image is justified by the autonomous ops thesis:  
Hull retained, island eliminated. The reference's angular stealth profile is the right starting point — tumblehome hull, faceted bow, split stern. But the traditional island superstructure is gone entirely. A flush-mounted C2 diamond replaces it. No human LSOs, no visual observation deck, no mast — just a SATCOM/data node embedded in the deck surface. That's a 94% RCS reduction right there and it's the single loudest design statement on the blueprint.  
VTOL grid replaces the runway. This is the core structural argument. V-BAT and X-BAT are tail-sitter VTOL — they don't need 300m of runway, catapults, or arresting gear. That entire mechanical system (the most complex and failure-prone part of a carrier) is replaced by 8 marked pads: 4 launch forward, 4 recovery aft. Simultaneous 4-drone launch/recovery pushes sortie rate to 32/hr sustained — roughly 3x what a Nimitz does with manned aircraft.  
Edge compute farm amidships. This is the Shield AI play. Onboard swarm autonomy means the drones don't need persistent BLOS comms to a remote ground station. The Hivemind inference stack runs on-hull. Ties directly into your V-JEPA cognitive load gate work from TAK-H — the ship is the swarm brain.  
Crew: 45\. Zero deck crew. Automated drone handling through two elevators feeding a 3-tier modular hangar below deck. The crew is there for ship operations and maintenance — not for air operations at all.

The side elevation tells the real story of why this ship works.  
The freeboard argument. The profile is absurdly low — 5m from waterline to deck edge, maybe 18m total hull depth. A Nimitz stands 20m from waterline to flight deck alone, before the island adds another 40m of vertical radar signature. This ship doesn't need that height because there's no manned aircraft needing launch/recovery clearance overhead. V-BATs lift straight up, rotate, and leave. The entire vertical profile collapses.  
Three-tier hangar logic. With no catapult machinery eating the first 60m of hull, and no arresting gear consuming the aft section, the entire interior becomes usable drone storage. Three full tiers running almost the complete length of the ship, split into forward (launch-assigned) and aft (recovery/rearm-assigned) sections with dedicated elevators for each. The drone silhouettes in the cross-section are stored vertical — tail-sitter position — which is the actual V-BAT stowage geometry and packs dramatically tighter than folded-wing manned aircraft.  
The cross-section reveals the tumblehome. 8° inward rake from waterline to deck — same geometry as DDG-1000. The beam narrows as it rises, deflecting radar energy downward instead of back to the emitter. Combined with the flat deck and eliminated island, the ship reads as a low flat wedge from any angle.  
Compute farm sits midship at the structural center — maximum survivability for the swarm brain. If the bow or stern takes damage, the Hivemind inference stack stays online.

The hangar deck is the operational thesis of the whole ship, drawn out as architecture.  
One-way conveyor logic. Forward cells feed to forward elevator for launch. Recovered drones come down the aft elevator into aft cells. Between them: the midship service zone with battery swap, weapons loading, and compute. Drones cycle forward-to-aft through the ship like product through a factory. No conflicting traffic patterns, no two-way corridors, no deck crew directing traffic. The handling system is automated rail — think Amazon fulfillment center logic applied to munitions.  
The CIC answers Humr directly. The bow compartment isn't a traditional combat information center — it's a human-machine teaming space staffed by 12 of the 45-person crew, the professionalized robotics warfare rating he describes. This is where DoDD 3000.09 compliance lives physically in the ship: legal review operators, engagement authority chains, the human judgment layer over autonomous targeting. The swarm runs itself operationally via the edge compute node midship, but every engagement decision routes through the CIC for human oversight.  
60 cells per section × 2 sections \= 120 per tier. This is H1 — the uppermost tier directly below the flight deck. H2 and H3 replicate the same layout below, giving surge capacity or maintenance rotation depth. At any given time, H1 is the active tier with hot-loaded drones ready for the elevators. H2 and H3 hold reserves, maintenance-in-progress airframes, and cold storage.  
The 90-second battery swap is the sortie rate multiplier. A recovered X-BAT comes down the aft elevator, gets racked, swaps battery and payload in under two minutes, and re-queues for launch via the forward conveyor. That's what gets you to 32 sustained sorties per hour.
